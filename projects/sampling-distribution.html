Sampling Distribution of the Mean

Date
June 2026
Context
Summer research-intensive bootcamp, NYU
Tools

Overview

This R Notebook is a simulation-based exploration of one of the most important ideas in statistics: the sampling distribution of the mean and the Central Limit Theorem (CLT).

The population is drawn from an exponential distribution — heavily right-skewed, nothing like a bell curve. That choice is intentional: it makes the CLT's effect visually dramatic. Even though the underlying data is skewed, the distribution of sample means converges toward a normal distribution as sample size grows.

What This Project Demonstrates

  • Central Limit Theorem — sample means drawn from a skewed exponential population become approximately normal as n increases, shown across 1,000 simulated samples at n = 5, 30, and 100.
  • Standard error formula — the observed spread of sample means matches the theoretical prediction SE = σ/√n closely at every sample size.
  • Unbiasedness — all three sampling distributions center on the true population mean regardless of sample size.
  • Diminishing returns — halving the standard error requires quadrupling the sample size, illustrated with a dedicated SE vs. n curve.
  • ggplot2 visualization — faceted histograms, overlaid density curves, annotated reference lines, and a custom warm color palette.

Key Takeaways

  • Larger samples produce narrower, taller sampling distributions — more data means more precise estimates of the population mean.
  • The standard error shrinks as √n, not n — there are real diminishing returns to collecting more data.
  • The shape of the sampling distribution converges to normal regardless of the population's shape, which is why so many statistical tests assume normality even when raw data isn't.
